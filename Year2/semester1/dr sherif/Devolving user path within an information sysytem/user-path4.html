Introduction to Co-Design

To co-design a product or service, you must focus on systematically aligning your offering's features with the specific, deeply understood needs of your target customers through collaborative processes.

This approach, often utilizing tools like the Value Proposition Canvas, ensures you create a compelling value proposition and the right functionalities.

Key Principle: The co-design process emphasizes involving stakeholders, including customers and internal teams, throughout development to ensure the final product or service delivers genuine value.

Core Components of Co-Design

Customer Understanding

  • Identify customer jobs to be done
  • Map customer pains and frustrations
  • Discover desired gains and outcomes
  • Involve customers throughout the process

Value Creation

  • Design products and services that fit
  • Create effective pain relievers
  • Develop compelling gain creators
  • Iterate based on feedback

Understanding Your Target Customer

The first step in co-design is developing a deep understanding of your target customer through three key elements:

1. Identify Customer Jobs

Determine the tasks customers are trying to accomplish across three dimensions:

  • Functional Jobs: Practical tasks and activities they need to complete (e.g., "get to work on time," "create a professional presentation")
  • Social Jobs: How they want to be perceived by others (e.g., "be seen as environmentally conscious," "appear professional to colleagues")
  • Emotional Jobs: How they want to feel (e.g., "feel confident," "experience peace of mind," "avoid stress")

2. Map Customer Pains

Document the negative experiences, frustrations, obstacles, and risks customers encounter when trying to get their jobs done:

  • Undesired outcomes and problems
  • Obstacles that prevent them from getting started
  • Risks they fear (financial, social, technical)
  • Frustrations with current solutions
  • Time wasted or inefficiencies

3. Identify Customer Gains

List the benefits, positive outcomes, and delightful surprises customers expect or desire:

  • Required gains (must-haves for the solution to work)
  • Expected gains (relatively basic expectations)
  • Desired gains (go beyond expectations but not essential)
  • Unexpected gains (exceed customer expectations)

Design the Value Map (Your Offering)

Once you understand your customer profile, design your offering to address their specific needs:

1. List Products & Services

Detail the specific tangible and intangible items you offer that help customers complete their jobs. Be concrete and specific about what you're providing.

2. Describe Pain Relievers

Explicitly state how your offerings eliminate or reduce specific customer pains.

  • Focus on the most intense pains first
  • Be specific about which pains you're addressing
  • Explain the mechanism of pain relief
  • Prioritize based on customer importance

3. Outline Gain Creators

Explain how your offerings produce the desired customer gains:

  • Focus on gains most relevant to the customer
  • Address required and expected gains first
  • Add desired gains for differentiation
  • Include unexpected gains for delight

Achieve "Fit" and Articulate the Value Proposition

Ensure your pain relievers and gain creators directly address the most significant pains and gains of your customer profile. This "fit" is the foundation of a strong value proposition.

Craft a clear, concise value proposition statement that explains:

"For [target customer], our [product/service] is a [category] that [solves specific problem] by [key features/differentiation], enabling [benefit/gain]"

Prototype, Test, and Iterate

  • Use prototypes or minimal viable products (MVPs) to gather feedback from real customers
  • Iterate the design and messaging based on customer responses
  • Achieve problem-solution fit, then product-market fit
  • Avoid generic claims; use the customer's own language to ensure the proposition resonates authentically

Example Co-Designed Product

A "Smart" Reusable Coffee Cup Service

By applying the co-design process, we can develop a smart reusable coffee cup system targeting environmentally conscious commuters.

Value Proposition:
"For environmentally conscious commuters, our smart reusable coffee cup service is a sustainable solution that eliminates single-use cup waste by offering high-tech, location-aware cups, enabling a seamless, zero-waste coffee experience without the hassle of washing or carrying a cup."

Functionalities & Features

The functionalities are designed to address specific customer jobs, pains, and gains identified during the co-design phase:

Location-Aware Mobile App

  • Find nearby participating cafes
  • Track personal environmental impact
  • Receive location-based reminders
  • View shareable achievements

RFID-Enabled Cups & Hubs

  • Easy automatic check-out and return
  • Seamless cafe station integration
  • No manual tracking required
  • Real-time cup availability

Automated Sanitization System

  • Installed in participating cafes
  • Industrial-grade cleaning
  • Manages cup hygiene automatically
  • Digital hygiene verification

Subscription Management Portal

  • Manage subscription plan
  • View sustainability data
  • Track cost savings over time
  • Access usage analytics

Pains Addressed & Gains Created

Pains Addressed

Hassle of Carrying / Washing

The service provides drop-off points at participating cafes, eliminating personal cleaning and transport responsibilities.

Forgetting the Cup at Home

Location-aware app notifications remind users to take a cup or indicate the nearest drop-off/pickup location.

Environmental Guilt

A clear, measurable impact tracker in the app shows exact waste prevented and carbon saved, giving users tangible evidence of their positive impact.

Hygiene Concerns

Industrial-grade sanitization at cafe hubs ensures a clean cup every time, which the app confirms via a digital seal for peace of mind.

Gains Created

Convenience

The seamless "grab and go" functionality matches the ease of disposable cups while being environmentally responsible.

Status & Social Good

Users feel good about their contribution and gain social status as conscious consumers, a value reflected in the app's shareable achievements.

Cost Savings

A subscription model eventually proves more cost-effective than daily disposables, highlighted transparently in the app.

Environmental Impact

Measurable reduction in waste and carbon footprint, with clear data visualization showing personal contribution to sustainability.

Produce Recommendations

Based on user test results, ergonomic recommendations for web developers focus on minimizing cognitive load, ensuring interface consistency, and optimizing responsiveness to create an intuitive, efficient, and comfortable user experience.

Translating User Test Data into Ergonomic Recommendations

User testing provides valuable insights into how real users interact with a product, revealing pain points and areas of confusion. A web developer can translate this feedback into specific ergonomic adjustments:

1. Identify Pain Points

Observe where users hesitate, make errors, or express frustration. These "friction points" highlight non-ergonomic elements, such as confusing navigation or cluttered layouts.

2. Analyze Behavior Patterns

Look for recurring issues across multiple user sessions. For example, if several users struggle to find a specific feature, it indicates a significant ergonomic problem with the information architecture or visual hierarchy.

3. Collect Qualitative and Quantitative Data

Gather both subjective feedback (user satisfaction ratings, comments) and objective metrics (task completion time, error rates). This helps prioritize which issues to address first based on their impact and frequency.

4. Prioritize Fixes

Use a framework to weigh the impact of an issue against the effort required to fix it. Focus on high-impact issues first to deliver the most value.

5. Iterate and Re-test

Implement changes based on the findings, and then conduct further testing to validate that the changes work as intended and do not introduce new problems.

Key Ergonomic Recommendations

1. Reduce Cognitive Load

The system should be intuitive and straightforward, requiring minimal mental effort from the user. User testing often reveals areas of confusion or complex flows.

Actionable Recommendation:

  • Simplify navigation paths and information architecture
  • Use clear, descriptive language for links and labels that reflect real-world concepts
  • Maintain consistency across the entire site to build user familiarity
  • Reduce the number of choices presented at once
  • Group related information logically

2. Optimize Visual Presentation and Accessibility

Design choices directly impact eye strain and visual comfort.

Actionable Recommendation:

  • Ensure a sufficient contrast ratio (at least 4.5:1 for normal text) for all text elements
  • Incorporate features like a dark mode option to reduce eye fatigue during prolonged use in low light
  • Use legible, sans-serif fonts
  • Allow users to resize text up to 200% without loss of content
  • Provide clear visual hierarchy through size, color, and spacing

3. Enhance Feedback Mechanisms

Users need to know that their actions are being processed and what the outcome is. A lack of feedback can lead to frustration and errors.

Actionable Recommendation:

  • Provide immediate and understandable system feedback for user actions
  • Use clear error messages that explain the problem and suggest a solution
  • Show loading indicators for processes that take time
  • Confirm successful actions with visual or textual feedback
  • Make system status visible at all times

Additional Ergonomic Best Practices

4. Ensure Responsiveness and Adaptability

Users access web systems from a variety of devices (desktops, tablets, smartphones). The interface should adapt seamlessly to different screen sizes and orientations.

Actionable Recommendation:

  • Implement responsive design principles with flexible layouts
  • Use appropriately sized touch targets for various devices (minimum 44x44 pixels)
  • Test the system across multiple platforms to ensure consistent experience
  • Optimize for different input methods (mouse, touch, keyboard)
  • Ensure content reflows naturally on different screen sizes

5. Minimize Physical Interaction Effort

For certain systems, physical interactions like excessive typing or specific click paths can cause strain over time.

Actionable Recommendation:

  • Minimize the number of steps required to complete a task (e.g., checkout or registration process)
  • Consider suggesting default values for common inputs
  • Use single-click actions for frequent tasks to reduce repetitive motion
  • Implement auto-fill and auto-complete where appropriate
  • Provide keyboard shortcuts for power users
  • Allow users to save preferences to avoid repeated inputs

Summary: The Ergonomic Design Cycle

  • Conduct thorough user testing with diverse participants
  • Identify pain points and behavior patterns
  • Prioritize issues based on impact and frequency
  • Implement ergonomic recommendations systematically
  • Re-test to validate improvements
  • Iterate continuously based on ongoing feedback

By following this cycle, web developers can create systems that are not only functional but also comfortable, efficient, and enjoyable to use over extended periods.
